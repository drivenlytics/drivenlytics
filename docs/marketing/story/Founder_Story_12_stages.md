## 1. Ordinary World

Who were you before all this? Set the scene. Where you came from,
what life looked like, what you were doing.

- Like you, I once had a J.O.B. And like all jobs. I worked too fast. I worked too hard. I was competitive. Work was not for me. People wanted to be lazy, weren't thankful for the opportunity. I only knew one mode — work. And work quickly. Before incubators and the startup seen, I was filing for patents. Bought magazines to Business Magazine. Before YouTube channels, before th internet was what it is today.

## 2. Call to Adventure

What disrupted the ordinary? The moment, the problem, the opportunity
that showed up and changed everything.

- Eventually, the corporate world sickened to me, and I left it behind. My inate ability to sell, a proven long-term success record, gave me the confidence to venture out on my own. Which I did successfully. 

Long-term plan was to build my own business. But, I didn't want to take shortcuts. I wanted solid footing to allow me to scale in any direction. I taught myself marketing, the legendary core skills of direct-response copywriting. Eventually, clocking out after 5 yrs to build a brandable business in hardware. 

Harder than software — most definitely. But hardwork isn't new to me. If you're in it for the long-run does it matter if you work too hard? 

I knew nothing of pitch decks, of meeting a VC, of putting a team together with no funding. I had holes in my marketing knowledge. Strong base, but at the time, didn't know SEO, didn't know ads. So, I learned it. 

And the process wasn't easy, but like you I was determined. And succeeded. 

I walked out full-stack on more than just marketing. 

The list is long. My innate and rare talent for design and sales combined with my analytical skills (background in finance and accounting) allowed me to progress far and probably go deeper than I should have. 

But I chase excellence and that crave for knowledge is keeps me up late at night and up early in the morning - like a constant train in motion. Wanting more sleep, but feeling not the need, but the fuel for more work to be done and learnings to be consumed.

The point of no return. The decision you made and what you left behind.

Understanding high-performance is only achieved with high-focus and attention to detail. This process of doing work while a discovery allowed for more real work, transformative work that changed the outcomes. Outcomes that didn't need to pass through the suffering from death, but instead satisfaction of the goal and potential outcomes. Work and stress don't have to be the same thing.
