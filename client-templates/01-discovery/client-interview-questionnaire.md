# Client Interview Questionnaire
**Purpose:** Used on discovery calls to uncover deep customer motivation, brand meaning, and archetypal identity  
**Note:** This is a living document — expand with new techniques as we develop them

---

## The Laddering Technique (The "Why?" Method)

Move the client up the ladder from physical product attributes → objective benefits → subjective benefits → deep meanings and values. Do this by persistently asking "Why?"

**The Four Rungs of the Ladder:**

1. **Physical Attributes** — The literal, tangible features of the product
   *Example: Salty snacks are crisp, crunchy, and angular*

2. **Objective Benefits** — The practical, functional outcome the attributes deliver
   *Example: Salty snacks require active chewing, engage the hands, stimulate the senses*

3. **Subjective Benefits** — The personal or emotional experience that results
   *Example: Salty snacks "spice things up," provide a "kick," set a party tone*

4. **Deep Meanings or Values** — The core human motivation or archetypal drive
   *Example: Salty snacks are an antidote to boredom, permission to test limits, freedom to let down one's reserve*

**How to run it in the interview:**

Ask the client what their customer wants → then ask "Why?" at each answer until you reach the deep value.

> *Example:*
> Client: "My customer wants a reliable car." (Attribute)
> You: "Why does that matter to them?"
> Client: "To get to work safely." (Objective benefit)
> You: "Why is that important?"
> Client: "To provide for their family." (Subjective benefit)
> You: "What does that mean to them at the deepest level?"
> Client: "They're driven by a need to protect and create stability." (Deep value → **Ruler motivation**)

**Important:** The same product can ladder to different deep values for different audiences.

> *Banking example:*
> - **Upscale consumers** → saving money → accumulating wealth → self-determination and freedom to do what they want, when they want (**Ruler + Explorer**)
> - **Middle-class consumers** → saving money → accumulating wealth → holding things together for the family, preserving stability (**Caregiver + Regular Guy**)

## Ethnographic Questions (Observing Real Life)

Go beyond demographics. Act like a cultural anthropologist to understand the "inherent drama" of the customer's daily life. Ask your client:

- How does your prospect decorate their home, and what is in their cupboards?
- When is their household most tense, and when is it most relaxed?
- What are the interactions between family members like?
- What in their home is most expressive of their values — or what would they run back to save in a fire?
- Is your brand's role meant to be purely **functional** for them, or is it **value expressive** (allowing them to express who they are to the world)?

---

## Life Stages and Dormant Urges

A customer's deepest motivation is often not what they are currently doing — but what they *yearn* to do. Ask your client:

- What is your customer's exact stage in the family life cycle?
  *(e.g., younger singles, early "full nest" with kids under 6, later "full nest," empty nesters, solitary survivors)*
- Beneath their current stressful roles, what are the **dormant urges** that speak to the person they truly want to become?
  *Example: A harried "Ruler" soccer mom who secretly yearns for the freedom to be creative → dormant **Creator** urge → explains why Martha Stewart appeals to her*
- Which fundamental human needs are being **well served** in their lives right now?
- Which fundamental human needs are being **underserved**?
  *Example: In a high-achievement culture where mastery is overserved, deep connection and belonging are underserved → gives power to Regular Guy and Lover brands*

---

## Notes for the Call

- Use laddering throughout — don't accept surface-level answers
- When a client describes their customer, always push to the deep value
- Ethnographic questions reveal things surveys never will — paint the picture of a day in their customer's life
- Dormant urges are often more powerful than current behavior — that's where brand loyalty lives
- A brand can ladder to different values for different audience segments — that's not a problem, it's a positioning decision
