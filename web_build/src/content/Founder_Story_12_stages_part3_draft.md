# The Founder

> [One-line hook]

---

## 1. Ordinary World

Every morning I put on a suit and walked into the stock exchange tower. I was selling mutual funds wholesale to the third-party broker dealer channel — wholesale banking. Corporate. Structured. A scoreboard with a number on it.

I was competitive. I was good at it. But underneath the suit, I was an entrepreneur trying to fit into a square peg hole. Raised by Asian parents with the expectation of title. Friends all following the same path. So I followed the herd. It didn't work — not because I couldn't perform, but because I could never fall in line. I had different values built on work hard and a no bullshit attitude. In short, my drum beat differently. And I knew it.

---

## 2. Call to Adventure

I did the job. I won. But there was no meaning to it. No value at the end of the day. Just a number on a scoreboard.

Then the team vp of sales lead who hired me was switched out. The new one was underperforming and placing the blame on me. Everyone in the room knew he couldn't perform. And I knew I was done.

I decided to pursue my entrepreneurial desires — completely in left field — in online marketing. This was an older on the cusp of Gen X crossing into the digital age. I knew nothing about it. But I had ideas that were validated by unicorn businesses. I had vision. I had creativity. I simply lacked the skills.

I forged ahead anyways with the desire to learn as I progressed and grow. That's what I really wanted I look back. 

---

## 3. Refusal of the Call

Like most people who weren't brought up in the technical age, there was a large amount of hesitancy. Clearly the lack of knowledge. The lack of financial security - a monthly salary. The identity I'd be walking away from into an unknown new situation.

It was a big change and I knew it wouldn't be easy. I refused many time before.... 

But this time despite the fear being real. I pushed ahead... 

---

## 4. Meeting the Mentor

I knew LACK of leadership would determine my ceiling of success. And I didn't want a ceiling. So I invested in myself — took a leadership course and started down that path.

Then I took my natural strengths — a people person who consistently excelled in sales — and applied them to the area of marketing that would deliver the most results. I niched down. And I successfully delivered - online.

Within three months I was working for some of the biggest direct-response marketers in the world. People building eight-figure businesses. After that, job offers and opportunities followed consistently.

A few people took a chance on me. And for that I am grateful. I delivered. But with every project, the pressure to keep delivering felt greater. Some supported me. Some tested me. Some tried to take from me. And when you do something that hard, you find out who's with you and who isn't.

The repetition of getting better became the teacher. The process of doing hard things, iterating, and improving made achieving the goal feel not just possible — but inevitable.

---

## 5. Crossing the Threshold

The whole point of getting into online marketing was so I could start my online business. Build something for myself. Pursue and fulfil my dreams.

I left the salary behind. I left the stability behind. I left the version of myself that wore a suit every day and called it a career. The embedded programming thought I was going somewhere... 

The change signified I was willing to take the long route to build the foundation for long-term success.

That was 10 years ago.

---

## 6. Tests, Allies & Enemies

Later, as I exited the freelance and consulting world I'd worked so hard to build, I knew I needed to empty the cup. I didn't want tinted glasses forcing me toward quick wins or early success to further handcuff me. I wanted a journey that would deliver long-term happiness.

So I aligned my passion for health and wellness with initially hardware — to bring real value to a market at scale.

You build allies. You encounter enemies. Without a doubt.

---

## 7. Approach

My approach was hardened by my work ethic. I adapted through persistence.

Before leaving the consulting world, I was doing workouts that most people wouldn't attempt. Every second day: 800 jumping jacks as a warm-up. Then 200 jumping jack burpees — 20 jumping jacks, 10 push-ups, no break, under 9 minutes. Then 300 lunges. Then core work. Then strength training. I did this working 8 PM to 5 AM.

I hated those days. I did them anyway.

Because in business, everything goes wrong. Almost all the time. It always wants to pull you off course, create chaos, throw problems at you when you least expect it. You have to persist day in and day out. You have to have mental toughness.

Work ethic isn't just about hours. It's about doing the hard thing when you don't want to — and doing it consistently until it becomes who you are.

I understand that. And I understand you.

---

## 8. Ordeal, Death & Rebirth

I've worked with people who lied to me. Who wasted my time. Who made promises and made excuses.

It was heartbreaking.

I had to make the tough calls. Move on. Maintain relationships where possible. But keep going toward the goal regardless.

What I came to understand in that process was this: work and stress don't have to be the same thing. The suffering doesn't come from the work — it comes from misaligned expectations, wrong people, and a lack of clarity on what needs to get done and why.

When I let the painful parts die, I made room for something better. Clarity. Focus. The right people.

---

## 9. Reward, Seizing the Sword

Eventually the reward came — not in a contract or a number, but in the relationships I'd built. Supportive entrepreneurs who believed in the vision, helped me build an early-stage prototype, and pushed me forward when the path wasn't clear.

I couldn't have done that without them.

And throughout all of it, I accumulated something more valuable than any single result: a full understanding of what it actually takes to build something from nothing.

The hiring. The firing. The legal. The sleepless nights. The moments you wonder if you made the right call. The loneliness of the decision that only you can make.

I know that world because I've lived it.

I get you.

---

## 10. The Road Back

It was at that point I realized what I'd built.

Over eight years I'd developed a rare combination of skills — data, sales psychology, brand, copy, design, analytics — and learned to apply them together in a way that most people can't. Not because the individual skills are hard to find, but because almost nobody holds all of them and knows how to make them work as one.

Words that sell. Stories that connect. Data that drives decisions. Systems that deliver.

I'd applied this for myself, for clients, and I knew it could help thousands of entrepreneurs who are out there doing the hard work without the tools or the guidance to make it land.

So I came full circle. And I decided to stop keeping it to myself.

---

## 11. Resurrection

There will always be another road to walk.

And at some point, NO ONE WILL BELIEVE YOU. No one will want to help. People will doubt you. 100%. Because they don't understand — and they likely never will — because you're built different.

Look, entrepreneurs are crazy people. They have crazy ideas. That doesn't mean the ideas aren't worth pursuing. It means you have to prove them wrong.

The moment you bet on yourself is when you win. It's always been the case. Those milestones are possible.

---

## 12. Return with Elixir

Before working with me, clients are doing everything themselves — wearing every hat, guessing at strategy, spending money on execution that doesn't convert. After, they have clarity on their message, a system that works, and someone who genuinely cares whether it performs.

I'm doing what I love. In service of entrepreneurs who are out there doing something hard and deserve to have the right person in their corner.

I've been built — skill by skill, scar by scar — to deliver something that very few people can.

Where my unique skills overlap is where breakthroughs happen.

That's what I bring to your business.
