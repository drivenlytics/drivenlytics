# Uncovering Your Brand Meaning: 5-Step Process

**Source:** The Hero and The Outlaw — Margaret Mark & Carol S. Pearson  
**Purpose:** Client brand discovery framework for Drivenlytics engagements

---

## Step One: Searching for Brand Soul

Act as a "brand biographer" to uncover the brand's deepest essence. Investigate its history, why it was created, how customers relate to it, and its original positioning.

- **Who created the brand, and why?**
  *Example:* Henry Ford created the Ford brand and the assembly plant to make car ownership accessible to everyday Americans, reflecting the spirit of an American folk hero.

- **What was going on in the broader culture at the time?**
  *Example:* When the Volkswagen Beetle was introduced, a strong 1960s counterculture rejected the large, gas-guzzling, status-symbol cars favored by their parents, creating a need for an "un-car" that valued fuel efficiency and innocence.

- **How was it first positioned?**
  *Example:* Oil of Olay was initially positioned not as a moisturizer, but as a "beauty secret," using print ads that read like a woman-to-woman intimacy rather than mass-market sales pitches.

- **What was the best or most memorable communication ever created for it?**
  *Example:* Apple's famous 1984 commercial, which showed a young, athletic woman smashing the screen of a "Big Brother" figure, brilliantly cemented the brand's revolutionary Outlaw identity.

- **How have customers related to the brand over the years, and how do they relate to it now?**
  *Example:* Over the years, customers have related to Campbell's Soup not merely as a food item, but as a nostalgic, comforting expression of a mother's Caregiver love.

- **What within its essence or equity makes it stand out from the competition?**
  *Example:* When Saturn launched, it stood out from Detroit automakers by emphasizing the trustworthy, honest, hardworking Regular Guy values of its plant workers in rural Tennessee.

- **If there were a documentary film made about your brand, what would it be called, and what would its plot be?**
  *Example:* The documentary could be a creation story about two hackers in a garage who turn a small idea into a massive corporate revolution, illustrating the Magician or Outlaw spirit.

---

## Step Two: Searching for Brand Substance

Ensure the identified archetype is grounded in a physical, contemporary truth about the product or service. Examine functional characteristics and discover the "inherent drama" of how the product is actually used in everyday life.

- **What is the basis of the brand's relationship with its customers — is there a real relationship or just a series of transactions?**
  *Example:* Nordstrom accepts returns no questions asked, sometimes even for items bought at other stores, building a profound Caregiver relationship based on trust and legendary service rather than just transactional retail.

- **What is the character of the organization's internal employee policies and customer complaint policies?**
  *Example:* At FedEx, employees evaluate their own managers, demonstrating a Hero organizational culture that demands competitive high performance but simultaneously treats employees with fairness and respect.

- **Is the brand's role functional or value expressive for its users?**
  *Example:* A $10 watch is purely functional because it keeps accurate time, but a $15,000 Rolex is value expressive because it communicates the customer's status, lifestyle, or achievement.

- **Is use of the brand episodic or routine?**
  *Example:* Drinking coffee used to be a routine, functional habit, but Starbucks transformed it into an episodic, exotic mini-vacation and a statement of personal identity.

- **Do consumers tend to use the brand exclusively or as their dominant brand, or is the brand part of a portfolio of brands that consumers find equally acceptable?**
  *Example:* Apple Macintosh users often use the brand exclusively and exhibit loyalty bordering on fanaticism, whereas users of other PCs may find various brands equally acceptable.

- **What is the consumers' level of attachment to the brand?**
  *Example:* Harley-Davidson riders have such a high level of attachment to the brand's Outlaw lifestyle that they frequently tattoo the company's logo onto their bodies.

- **Are you trying to hold onto your current franchise, attract users who favor other brands, or expand use of the product category overall by way of attraction to your brand?**
  *Example:* Barnes & Noble expanded the use of the entire bookstore category by adding coffee shops and comfortable chairs, transforming bookstores from simple retail shops into community gathering places.

---

## Step Three: Finding the Competitive Leverage

Evaluate your competitive set to ensure your chosen archetype offers "significant and sustainable differentiation in the marketplace." Analyze the archetypes your competitors are using to find a unique identity or to tap into a deeper level of an archetype the category has overlooked.

- **Have any competitors stumbled into archetypal territory, and if so, which archetypes are they utilizing?**
  *Example:* In the highly competitive telecom industry, MCI successfully stumbled into Caregiver territory by launching the "Friends and Family" program, which emphasized emotional connections and briefly blindsided AT&T.

- **How well are the competitors supporting and living up to their archetypes?**
  *Example:* Levi's has drifted confusingly from Outlaw to Hero to Explorer to Regular Guy, losing its market share, whereas Nike strongly and consistently lives up to its Hero archetype.

- **At what level are the competitors expressing the archetype, and is there an opportunity to move to a deeper, more relevant, or differentiating level?**
  *Example:* Competitors in teen apparel might all be using the superficial Explorer level of the "rebellious outsider," creating an opportunity for your brand to use a deeper level of the Explorer, such as "the seeker of inner truth and authenticity."

- **Is everyone in the category expressing the same one or two archetypes, creating an opportunity for a truly new archetype in the category?**
  *Example:* When almost all other coffee brands were marketing as homey, domestic Caregivers (like Folgers), Starbucks created an opportunity by adopting an Explorer identity focused on exotic, foreign lands.

---

## Step Four: Know Your Customer

Confirm that the archetype will be powerfully relevant to your target audience. Instead of relying on superficial lifestyle traits, examine the customer's developmental life stage, family life cycle, and deeper psychological needs or dormant urges.

- **What is your prospect's individual stage in life and family life cycle (e.g., younger or older singles, early or later "full nest", solitary survivors)?**
  *Example:* The prospect might be a forty-something mother in the "full nest" stage, constantly chauffeuring kids and holding down a job, which forces her to adopt a highly controlling "Ruler" mindset to keep her family's daily life from falling into chaos.

- **What are the deeper "burgeoning" identities or dormant urges of your customers that speak to the person they truly want to become?**
  *Example:* That same harried "Ruler" soccer mom might have a dormant "Creator" urge to express herself artistically and beautify her surroundings, which explains why Martha Stewart's brand appeals to her so powerfully.

- **Which fundamental human needs are being well served at this point in our individual or collective history, and which are being underserved?**
  *Example:* In a highly driven, fast-paced society where individual achievement and mastery are overserved, the need for deep, intimate connection and belonging is widely underserved, giving immense power to Regular Guy or Lover brands.

---

## Step Five: Staying on Course — Managing the "Brand Bank"

Once the identity is established, carefully nourish it by managing your "brand bank." Ensure that any marketing actions — like price promotions or product extensions — make "deposits" of meaning to reinforce the brand's archetype, rather than "withdrawals" that deplete its equity.

- **Are your planned marketing actions (like price promotions or product extensions) enhancing and nourishing the essential archetypal meaning of the brand to make "deposits," or are they taking "withdrawals" that trade on it?**
  *Example:* McDonald's makes a "withdrawal" from its brand equity when it runs an opportunistic short-term price promotion, but it makes a compensatory "deposit" into its Innocent brand bank by supporting the Ronald McDonald Houses for children.

- **Have you invested heavily in creating strong, consistent archetypal associations over time before assuming you can cash in on them through brand extensions?**
  *Example:* Ralph Lauren built such a consistent, unshakeable Ruler/Aristocrat identity over decades that he could successfully cash in on that equity by extending his name to home furnishings and the Polo Sport line.

- **Do you have an infrastructure in place to communicate that meaning to all stakeholders and to any or all marketing consultants, advertising firms, or other people involved in marketing decisions?**
  *Example:* In its early days, Oil of Olay required all new company employees and advertising agency partners to read a "white paper" that explicitly detailed the special history, secrets, and magical culture of the brand.

- **Are you viewing your customers less as just consumers to be convinced to buy your product, and more as constituents in an ecosystem?**
  *Example:* Microsoft achieved massive growth not just by selling software to consumers, but by viewing IBM and other PC makers as constituents in a shared ecosystem, establishing partnerships that ensured Windows was preloaded on almost every machine.
